How many mistakes are we supposed to make?

Posted on Dec 08, 2018 by Klaus Paul 2 mins read

This article is from an email newsletter I received from Mike Cohn. I am copying the article to share with those that have not yet found out about that.

It seems like we spend a lot of our lives avoiding mistakes. No one wants to make a mistake. And in business, mistakes cost us time and money (often both).

But sometimes the effort spent avoiding a mistake can exceed the cost of making the mistake. Let’s run through a few scenarios to see why that is.

Suppose you run a year-long project. At the end of the year, you and your teammates look back at the project and realize you only made four mistakes.

Not bad.

Except each mistake set the project back three months. Uh-oh.

Suppose instead you look back at a different year-long project. And you’re extremely pleased there were no big, three-month mistakes.

In fact, no mistake cost the team more than a day of rework to recover from. Time to crack open the champagne and celebrate!

Not so fast. No mistake cost more than a day but there were 200 one-day mistakes. Put the champagne back on ice.

These are each clearly bad situations. And your takeaway might be that you need to spend more time deliberating before making future decisions.

So let’s suppose you and your teammates do that. And at the end of the next year, you really do celebrate because you made absolutely no mistakes, large or small, during the entire year.

This is better than the excessive mistakes of the prior examples. But I’d argue you probably went too far.

Avoiding every mistake, no matter how small, probably required you to do a lot of upfront thinking before making even simple decisions. It is quite likely you and the team spent more effort (and money) avoiding certain problems than it would have cost to simply deal with them.

How many mistakes should you make in a year?

I have no idea.

But I do know the optimal number is not zero. If you never make a mistake, you are slowing progress by doing too much upfront thinking. Realizing this will help your team succeed with agile,

Mike
