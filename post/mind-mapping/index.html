“Oh Ellie, what have I gotten myself into…?”

Mind mapping

Posted on Sep 19, 2018 by Klaus Paul 3 mins read

In one of my previous projects, the priority for one of the iterations was to size the effort for different features of the system, in order for business to make an informed decision on which option to select as priority for the next fiscal year. Why were we doing that deserves its own post (or series of).

The task seemed daunting: we were tasked to sizing the effort not for one sprint, but for several ones, months in advance.

At first, I was at loss as to how to visualize all the possible things that I could thing of, so I could at least go to the team sizing exercise with some sort of idea as to what to size.

Perhaps open up a new Word document and start listening everything that came to mind? No. Too cumbersome to move and group ideas. What about Excel? Working with cells does seem more practical than working with a Word document. Perhaps a Visio diagram? Again, too much power for the purpose: easily capture thoughts, group them together, show which ones are related, and so on. Then I remembered: mind maps.

Mind maps, according to definition found in Wikipedia:

A mind map is a diagram used to visually organize information. A mind map is hierarchical and shows relationships among pieces of the whole. It is often created around a single concept, drawn as an image in the center of a blank page, to which associated representations of ideas such as images, words and parts of words are added.

Here’s an example of what a mind map might look like. The look-n-feel may change from map to map, but the basic layout is always the same: the main concept in the center and branching out of it, similar to branches in a tree, all the related terms, concepts, ideas, etc., that pertain to that main concept. Below I have two examples. Try this search for more examples.

Using my preferred mind mapping tool, XMind, I started brainstorm ideas around the main topic: size the product features necessary for first MVP.

Other mind mapping tools may be as efficient as, but I tried XMind first and fell in love with it. The tool allows for a very simple and efficient way to “dump” your thoughts and easily group and reorganized them as you know more and more about what you are planning.

I have also used mind mapping in another assignment where I was revamping a team’s knowledge base from a open-source yet discontinued Wiki platform to the company’s platform, Confluence. To track the progress of my analysis, I created a mind map around the team knowledge base concept. That really helped me in “view” the organization’s landscape: what groups managed the Confluence instances, how many instances, and so on.

If advice was good they would be sold, not given, but here it goes any way: give mind mapping a try. It may as well “revolutionize” how you approach organizing your work.
