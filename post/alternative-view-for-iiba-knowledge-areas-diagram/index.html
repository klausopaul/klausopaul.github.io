Alternative view for IIBA knowledge areas diagram

Posted on Sep 20, 2018 by Klaus Paul 2 mins read

For those who haven’t seen it yet, here’s one version of the current knowledge areas diagram, as per BABOK® v3.0, depicting the general relationships between each area:

The first time I saw it, I took it at face value and didn’t question the way the relationships are showed. It seemed to make sense: Business Analysis Planning & Monitoring is related to Strategy Analysis and Requirements Analysis and Design Definition.

This time I am committed to get my CBAP, and I look into this diagram in more detail. The initial goal was simple: memorize it for the exam.

In the course of that task, I noticed two things that bothered me:

  • What is the meaning of this arrow? Does it mean that Elicitation and Collaboration is related to both Strategy Analysis and Solution Evaluation? Or does it mean that it is related to the outputs of those two? What is this trying to convey?
![babok_ka_subsetquestion](/img/posts/babok_ka_subsetquestion.png)
  • That is not the only one missing relationship, but shouldn’t Business Analysis Planning & Monitoring be also related to Solution Evaluation?

The former “…describes the tasks that business analysts perform to organize and coordinate the efforts of business analysts and stakeholders., while the latter “…describes the tasks that business analysts perform to assess the performance of and value delivered by a solution…". To me this indicates they are related. Is the red line missing or did I get the diagram wrong?

![babok_ka_subsetquestion2](/img/posts/babok_ka_subsetquestion2.png)

According to BABOK®:

Tasks are grouped into knowledge areas. Business analysts perform tasks from all knowledge areas sequentially, iteratively, or simultaneously. The BABOK® Guide does not prescribe a process or an order in which tasks are performed.

What if then, the relationship diagram can be updated to clearly depict one simple concept from the above definition: ultimately, all knowledge areas are related.

For example, see the image below, the BACCM (Business Analysis Core Concept Model):

![BACCM (Business Analysis Core Concept Model)](https://proxy.duckduckgo.com/iu/?u=http%3A%2F%2Fmasteringbusinessanalysis.com%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2016%2F08%2FBACCM.jpg)

There is no ambiguity: every part is related to every other part; there is no implicit priority or flow.

My suggestion for depicting the relationships between the knowledge is this:

![An alternative view of the general relationships between IIBA knowledge areas](/img/posts/ka_diagram_kp.png)

I am not looking for it to be accepted as the formal one, but at least to me, at this point, it both makes more sense, and it is easier to remember, and aligns to the way the BACCM is laid out.
